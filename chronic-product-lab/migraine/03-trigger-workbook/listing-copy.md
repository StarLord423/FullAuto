## Description

Use this printable workbook to organize what was happening around migraine attacks: sleep, schedule, meals, hydration, caffeine, alcohol, stress, sensory exposure, weather, cycle timing, medication/support notes, and questions for your healthcare provider.

This workbook is designed for pattern noticing, not self-blame. It includes space to mark uncertainty, no clear pattern, and questions to discuss with a qualified healthcare professional.

## Who This Is For

This workbook is for people with migraine or chronic migraine who want to notice possible patterns without assuming every attack has a clear cause. It may be especially useful if you are preparing for a primary care, neurology, headache specialist, or follow-up appointment.

## What You Can Track

- Migraine attack timing, symptoms, aura, intensity, and impact
- Sleep and schedule changes
- Meals, hydration, caffeine, and alcohol notes
- Stress, letdown, bright light, screens, smells, sound, and overstimulation
- Weather, environment, travel, and air quality notes
- Cycle, hormonal, illness, neck tension, posture, or exertion context
- Medication/support timing, relief, side effects, and access questions
- Patterns that appeared more than once
- Things that did not show a clear pattern
- Questions to bring to a healthcare provider

## How to Use

1. Print the workbook or the pages you want to use.
2. Use the attack log for migraine days or individual attacks.
3. Use the context pages to collect notes over time.
4. Review several entries together before drawing conclusions.
5. Use the final page to summarize patterns, uncertainty, and questions for your healthcare provider.

## Important

This is a digital printable. No physical item will be shipped.

This printable is a personal tracking and organization tool. It is not medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Always talk with a qualified healthcare professional about symptoms, medications, or care decisions.

This workbook can help organize possible context around migraine attacks. It cannot prove what caused an attack, and it should not be used to make medication, diet, or care changes without a qualified healthcare professional.

If symptoms feel new, sudden, severe, or concerning, contact a qualified healthcare professional or emergency services.
