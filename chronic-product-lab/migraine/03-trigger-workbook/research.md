# Research Package: Migraine Pattern Workbook

## 1. Product Positioning

This product should be positioned as an 8- to 12-page printable workbook for noticing possible migraine patterns over time. It is not a promise to find a trigger, prevent attacks, or make migraine controllable. The safest and strongest angle is:

> A migraine pattern workbook for organizing possible context around attacks, reviewing what shows up repeatedly, and preparing clearer notes for a healthcare provider.

Use language like:

- "possible context"
- "patterns to discuss"
- "things I noticed"
- "what was happening before or around an attack"
- "questions for my healthcare provider"

Avoid language like:

- "find your migraine triggers"
- "identify the cause"
- "prevent attacks"
- "control your migraines"
- "eliminate triggers"

The product should feel practical, calming, and non-blaming. It should acknowledge that migraine is complex, that triggers vary by person, and that sometimes no clear pattern appears.

## 2. Buyer Situation

The buyer may have recurring migraine or chronic migraine and feel overwhelmed by advice to "track your triggers." They may have tried apps, food diaries, weather notes, or memory-based tracking and still feel unsure. They may be preparing for a primary care, neurology, headache specialist, or follow-up appointment and want a clearer way to explain what they have noticed.

Likely frustrations:

- They feel blamed when people talk about triggers as if migraine is simple.
- They cannot tell whether a food, smell, weather change, stress, or poor sleep caused an attack or was just nearby.
- They wonder whether food cravings, mood changes, fatigue, yawning, or sensory sensitivity were actually early migraine signs.
- They want to notice patterns without obsessing over every detail.
- They need enough structure to track consistently, but not a giant homework packet.
- They want a summary page to bring to a healthcare provider.

The workbook should reduce overwhelm by grouping possible context categories and giving the buyer permission to write "unknown," "not sure," or "no clear pattern."

## 3. Migraine-Specific Tracking Needs

The workbook should capture both migraine attacks and the surrounding context. It should not become a generic habit tracker.

Core migraine attack details:

- Date and day of week
- Start time and end time
- Duration
- Pain intensity
- Pain location and quality
- Aura or neurological symptoms
- Associated symptoms: nausea, vomiting, light sensitivity, sound sensitivity, smell sensitivity, fatigue, neck pain, dizziness/vertigo, brain fog, food cravings, chills/sweats, migraine hangover
- Migraine phase clues: prodrome, aura, headache, postdrome, unsure
- Medication/support used and timing
- Relief level
- Impact on work, school, caregiving, transport, screens, household tasks, or plans

Pattern/context details:

- Sleep timing, sleep quality, naps, disrupted sleep, oversleeping, shift changes
- Meals, delayed meals, skipped meals, hydration, caffeine changes, alcohol
- Stress, stress letdown, emotional strain, overexertion, recovery after busy periods
- Sensory exposure: bright light, glare, screens, flicker, strong smells, loud sound
- Weather/environment: heat, storms, pressure changes, humidity, smoke, air quality, travel, altitude
- Hormonal/cycle timing where relevant
- Medication/treatment timing, missed doses, medication frequency, side effects, barriers, refills
- Illness, neck tension, posture strain, travel, schedule changes, dehydration risk, unusual exertion

Important framing:

- Track what was happening; do not assume it caused the attack.
- Some "triggers" may be early symptoms of migraine already starting.
- Multiple factors may combine.
- Some attacks may have no obvious pattern.

## 4. Common Trigger Categories

Use "trigger category overview" as the buyer-facing title only if paired with careful explanation. Prefer "possible context categories" in the body.

Useful categories:

### Sleep and Schedule

- Too little sleep
- Too much sleep
- Irregular sleep
- Shift work
- Naps
- Travel or time-zone changes
- Weekend schedule changes
- Busy days followed by letdown

### Food, Caffeine, Hydration, and Alcohol

- Missed or delayed meals
- Hydration changes
- Caffeine increase, decrease, or timing change
- Alcohol, especially if the buyer already suspects it
- Highly individualized food notes
- Food cravings that may be prodrome rather than cause

Avoid food-fear framing. The workbook should not imply a restrictive diet or universal food triggers.

### Stress, Letdown, and Emotional Load

- Acute stress
- Ongoing stress
- Letdown after stress
- Grief, conflict, overstimulation, deadline pressure
- Recovery after high-demand periods

### Sensory Exposure

- Bright light
- Glare
- Screen exposure
- Flicker
- Strong smells
- Loud sound
- Crowded or overstimulating spaces

### Weather and Environment

- Barometric pressure changes
- Storms
- Heat
- Humidity
- Cold exposure
- Smoke or air quality
- Travel
- Altitude
- Indoor/outdoor environment changes

### Hormonal/Cycle Notes

- Menstrual-cycle timing where relevant
- Perimenopause/menopause notes if relevant
- Hormonal medication timing or changes only as something to discuss with a healthcare professional

### Medication/Treatment Context

- Acute medication timing
- Preventive medication notes
- Missed doses
- Side effects
- Refill/access barriers
- Frequency of acute medication use to discuss with a clinician
- Non-medication supports: rest, dark room, hydration, food, sleep, ice/heat, reduced light/sound

## 5. Common Medication and Treatment Tracking Categories

Medication pages should be neutral, factual, and discussion-oriented.

Track:

- Medication or support used
- Time used
- Relief level
- Time relief began
- Side effects or concerns
- Whether a second step was used
- Barriers: cost, refill timing, nausea, difficulty taking medication during aura or severe symptoms
- Questions for provider about medication timing, frequency, side effects, refills, or treatment plan

Do not:

- Tell users when to take medication.
- Tell users to stop, start, increase, decrease, combine, or avoid medication.
- Recommend supplements or specific treatments.
- Use medication-overuse language as a warning directive. Safer: "If you are concerned about how often you use acute medication, discuss it with a qualified healthcare professional."

## 6. Doctor-Visit Communication Needs

This workbook should produce a useful appointment summary, not just raw logs. The doctor discussion page should help the buyer explain:

- Time period tracked
- Number of migraine days or attack days, if known
- Possible context that appeared repeatedly
- Context that did not show a clear pattern
- Symptoms that changed or felt concerning
- Medication/support patterns and questions
- Daily-life impact
- What the buyer wants help understanding
- What they should track next

Include prompts:

- "Patterns I noticed more than once:"
- "Things I expected to matter but did not clearly match attacks:"
- "Questions I want to ask:"
- "What I want help understanding:"
- "What I should track before the next visit:"

Important: The summary should make it easy to say "I am not sure," because that is often clinically useful and emotionally safer than forcing conclusions.

## 7. Buyer Language and Phrases

Use language that feels validating and low-pressure:

- "This is not about blaming yourself."
- "You are collecting clues, not proving a cause."
- "Some attacks may not have an obvious pattern."
- "A possible trigger might also be an early migraine symptom."
- "Multiple small factors may stack together."
- "Use estimates if exact details are hard."
- "Skip anything that does not apply."
- "Bring this summary to your provider if it helps."
- "Write 'unknown' when you are not sure."

Buyer phrases that the product should respond to:

- "Everyone tells me to track triggers, but I do not know what counts."
- "I am scared to eat normal foods because I might cause a migraine."
- "Sometimes stress seems involved, but sometimes it does not."
- "Weather might be a pattern, but I cannot control it."
- "I need to explain this without rambling at my appointment."
- "I want to notice patterns without turning my life into a spreadsheet."

Tone:

- Calm
- Practical
- Non-blaming
- Not clinical
- Not fear-based
- Not overly inspirational

## 8. Existing Product Gaps to Avoid

Avoid these weaknesses:

- Generic trigger checklist with no migraine-specific symptom context.
- Food-policing pages that imply restrictive dieting.
- Pages that treat triggers as universal or simple.
- "Find your triggers" language without uncertainty.
- No place to mark "no clear pattern."
- No distinction between context, possible trigger, and prodrome.
- Too many daily habit fields that make the workbook feel like punishment.
- No doctor discussion page.
- Medication pages that imply treatment advice.
- Tiny writing areas.
- Heavy decorative design that prints badly.
- Missing disclaimer.

The final product should be useful even if the buyer discovers no obvious trigger pattern.

## 9. Medical Safety Boundaries

Required disclaimer:

> This printable is a personal tracking and organization tool. It is not medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Always talk with a qualified healthcare professional about symptoms, medications, or care decisions.

Recommended pattern disclaimer:

> This workbook can help you organize possible context around migraine attacks. It cannot prove what caused an attack, and it should not be used to make medication, diet, or care changes without a qualified healthcare professional.

Conservative urgent-symptom language:

> If symptoms feel new, sudden, severe, or concerning, contact a qualified healthcare professional or emergency services.

Hard rules:

- Do not claim to diagnose migraine or identify the cause of attacks.
- Do not claim to prevent, reduce, treat, cure, or control migraine.
- Do not promise that triggers can be found.
- Do not imply that the buyer is responsible for causing attacks.
- Do not recommend elimination diets.
- Do not recommend medication changes.
- Do not claim doctor approval.
- Do not use fake medical symbols, seals, or credentials.

## 10. Recommended Page List

Recommended 10-page workbook:

1. Cover / How to Use This Workbook
   - Purpose
   - How long to track
   - Permission to skip sections
   - "Clues, not blame" note
   - Disclaimer

2. Trigger Category Overview
   - Possible context categories
   - Difference between context, possible trigger, and early migraine clue
   - "No clear pattern" is valid

3. Migraine Attack Log
   - Date, time, duration, intensity
   - Aura, symptoms, phase clues
   - Medication/support used
   - Impact

4. Sleep and Schedule Log
   - Bed/wake time
   - Sleep quality
   - Schedule disruption
   - Travel or time-zone change
   - Notes about attack timing

5. Food, Caffeine, Hydration, and Alcohol Notes
   - Meals delayed/skipped
   - Hydration notes
   - Caffeine timing or change
   - Alcohol notes
   - Food notes without restrictive framing
   - "Could this have been prodrome?" prompt for cravings

6. Stress and Sensory Exposure Notes
   - Stress, letdown, emotional strain
   - Bright light, screens, glare, flicker
   - Strong smells, loud sound, overstimulation
   - What was unavoidable

7. Weather, Environment, and Travel Notes
   - Weather/pressure
   - Heat/humidity/cold
   - Smoke/air quality
   - Travel, altitude, environment change
   - What the buyer wants to ask about

8. Hormonal/Cycle and Body Context Notes
   - Cycle timing where relevant
   - Perimenopause/menopause notes if relevant
   - Illness, neck tension, posture, exertion
   - Skip-friendly language for buyers this does not apply to

9. Medication/Treatment and Relief Notes
   - What was used and when
   - Relief level
   - Side effects or barriers
   - Questions for provider
   - Note not to change medications based on workbook alone

10. Pattern Review and Doctor Discussion Page
   - What showed up repeatedly
   - What did not show a clear pattern
   - What may be early migraine symptoms
   - Questions for healthcare provider
   - What to track next

Optional if the builder needs 12 pages:

- Add a weekly pattern review page.
- Add a "summary for appointment" page separate from the pattern review.

Do not exceed 12 pages for this product.
