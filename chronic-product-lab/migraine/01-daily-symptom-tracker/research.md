# Research Package: Migraine Daily Symptom Tracker

## 1. Product Positioning

This product is a two-page printable for recording one migraine attack at a time. Page 1 should feel fast, calm, and practical enough to use during or shortly after an attack, when the buyer may have pain, light sensitivity, brain fog, nausea, or fatigue. Page 2 should provide expanded writing space for context, medication/support details, patterns, and appointment questions.

Position it as:

- A personal migraine log.
- A headache diary page.
- A doctor-visit communication aid.
- A low-friction way to capture symptoms, possible context, medication/support notes, relief, and impact.

Do not position it as:

- A diagnostic tool.
- A treatment tool.
- A cure or prevention method.
- A substitute for medical care.

Core buyer-facing promise:

> Capture the migraine details you may forget later, so you can notice patterns and communicate more clearly with your healthcare provider.

## 2. Buyer Situation

The buyer may be dealing with recurring migraine or chronic migraine and trying to remember what happened before, during, and after attacks. They may be preparing for a primary care, neurology, headache specialist, or follow-up appointment.

Likely frustrations:

- They forget details by the time they see a provider.
- They do not want a full journal when their head hurts.
- Phone apps may be too bright or annoying during an attack.
- Generic symptom trackers miss aura, sensory symptoms, and postdrome.
- Trigger tracking can feel overwhelming or self-blaming.
- They want something they can print, mark quickly, and bring to an appointment.

Page 1 should use checkboxes more than writing prompts. Page 2 should provide more blank space for handwritten notes without feeling sparse.

## 4. Common Trigger Categories

Use cautious language: "possible context" or "things I noticed" instead of "cause" or "trigger found." Migraine triggers are not always simple, predictable, or controllable.

Include these possible context categories:

- Sleep change
- Missed or delayed meal
- Hydration low
- Caffeine change
- Alcohol
- Stress
- Stress letdown
- Bright light
- Screen exposure
- Strong smells
- Loud sound
- Weather or barometric pressure change
- Hormonal or menstrual-cycle timing
- Exertion or exercise
- Travel
- Schedule disruption
- Food notes
- Medication timing notes

Buyer-sensitive framing:

> These notes are for pattern spotting, not self-blame.

## 5. Common Medication and Treatment Tracking Categories

Use blank, neutral fields. Do not recommend specific medication, dosing, supplements, or care changes.

Track:

- Medication or support used
- Dose or amount, if the user chooses to record it
- Time used
- Time relief began
- Relief level
- Side effects or notes
- Whether a second support step was used

Non-medication support options can include:

- Dark room
- Sleep/rest
- Hydration
- Ice or heat
- Reduced sound
- Reduced light
- Food/snack
- Other

Required safety framing:

> Record what happened. Do not change medications or care decisions based on this page alone. Talk with a qualified healthcare professional about medication questions.

## 6. Doctor-Visit Communication Needs

The tracker should help the buyer communicate:

- How often migraine attacks happen
- How long attacks last
- Whether aura occurs
- Which symptoms are most disruptive
- Whether symptoms affect work, school, caregiving, driving, sleep, or daily tasks
- What they used and whether it helped
- What felt new, different, severe, or concerning
- What questions they want to ask next time

Include healthcare-provider note sections:

- "Quick Notes to Share With My Healthcare Provider" on page 1.
- "Appointment Prep" and free notes on page 2.

Suggested prompts:

- "What felt different, new, severe, or especially disruptive?"
- "Questions I want to ask next time:"

## 7. Buyer Language and Phrases

Use plain, validating language. Avoid melodrama.

Useful phrases:

- "I forget everything once I am finally in the appointment."
- "I need something I can fill out when my head hurts."
- "Was this a trigger, or was it part of the migraine starting?"
- "I want to track patterns without blaming myself."
- "The migraine hangover is real."
- "Light, sound, and smells all hit differently."
- "I need a quick page I can bring to my doctor."
- "I want to remember what helped and what did not."
- "I cannot stare at an app during an attack."
- "I need checkboxes, not homework."

Tone guidelines:

- Calm
- Direct
- Not cutesy
- Not clinical
- Not fear-based
- Not overly inspirational

## 8. Existing Product Gaps to Avoid

Avoid these common weaknesses:

- Generic "symptom tracker" pages with migraine pasted into the title.
- No aura section.
- No sensory symptoms.
- No postdrome / migraine hangover section.
- No medication timing section.
- No relief tracking.
- Tiny handwriting spaces.
- Too many decorative elements.
- Heavy colors that print badly.
- Pages that imply the buyer can control migraine if they just find triggers.
- Overly long reflection questions.
- No doctor-visit notes.
- No medical disclaimer.

The final product should be more useful than a generic wellness tracker because it includes migraine-specific attack phases, aura, sensory symptoms, possible context, medication/support notes, and appointment communication.

## 9. Medical Safety Boundaries

Hard rules:

- Do not claim the tracker diagnoses migraine.
- Do not claim the tracker treats migraine.
- Do not claim the tracker prevents migraine.
- Do not claim the tracker reduces attacks.
- Do not imply trigger identification is guaranteed.
- Do not advise medication changes.
- Do not use "doctor-approved" unless a real qualified professional reviewed it.
- Do not use fake credentials, medical seals, or professional endorsements.

Required disclaimer:

> This printable is a personal tracking and organization tool. It is not medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Always talk with a qualified healthcare professional about symptoms, medications, or care decisions.

Conservative urgent-symptom language:

> If symptoms feel new, severe, sudden, or concerning, contact a qualified healthcare professional or emergency services.
