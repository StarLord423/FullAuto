# Research Package: Migraine Doctor Visit Prep Pack

## 1. Product Positioning

This product should be positioned as a personal appointment organization tool for people with migraine or chronic migraine. Its job is to help the buyer gather the details that are easy to forget during a short visit: frequency, symptoms, possible context, medication and treatment history, daily-life impact, goals for the appointment, and follow-up notes.

Safe positioning language:

- "Prepare clearer notes for a migraine appointment."
- "Bring a concise summary of symptoms, frequency, treatments tried, and questions."
- "Organize what you want to discuss with a healthcare provider."
- "Notice patterns to talk through with your clinician."

Avoid positioning it as a diagnostic tool, treatment planner, emergency guide, or trigger-finding promise.

## 2. Buyer Situation

The likely buyer is preparing for a primary care visit, neurology visit, headache specialist appointment, medication follow-up, disability/work accommodation conversation, or a visit after migraine symptoms have changed. They may feel rushed, dismissed, foggy, light-sensitive, overwhelmed by months of attacks, or unsure how to summarize scattered notes.

Useful emotional framing:

- "I do not want to blank when the appointment starts."
- "I need to explain how often this is happening without rambling."
- "I want my provider to understand the impact on work, school, caregiving, and daily life."
- "I have tried several medications or supports and need a clean list."
- "I want to ask better questions, not leave remembering what I forgot."

## 3. Migraine-Specific Tracking Needs

Appointment prep should include migraine-specific prompts rather than generic wellness fields. Important details include:

- Attack frequency by month or week.
- Number of migraine days versus headache days, when the buyer can estimate.
- Duration range: hours, one day, multiple days, or lingering postdrome.
- Pain location and quality: one-sided, both sides, behind eye, temple, neck, throbbing, pressure, stabbing, pulsing.
- Associated symptoms: nausea, vomiting, light sensitivity, sound sensitivity, smell sensitivity, fatigue, neck pain, brain fog, dizziness or vertigo.
- Aura and neurological symptoms: visual changes, blind spots, zigzags, flashing lights, tingling or numbness, speech/language difficulty, confusion, weakness, dizziness or vertigo.
- Migraine phases: prodrome, aura, headache, postdrome, or unsure.
- Possible context: sleep changes, missed meals, hydration, caffeine changes, stress/letdown, sensory exposure, weather/pressure changes, hormonal/cycle timing, travel, schedule disruption.
- Function and impact: missed work/school, canceled plans, caregiving limitations, reduced chores, driving limitations, screen intolerance, needing a dark/quiet room.
- Treatment history: acute medications, preventive medications, devices, supplements, physical therapy, injections, lifestyle supports, non-medication supports, side effects, partial relief, no relief, barriers such as cost or access.

## 4. Common Trigger Categories

The pack should use cautious language: "possible context" or "patterns to discuss," not "your triggers." Migraine attacks can be complex, and some apparent triggers may be early symptoms or coincidental patterns.

Common categories worth listing:

- Sleep changes or irregular schedule.
- Missed or delayed meals.
- Dehydration or hydration changes.
- Caffeine changes.
- Alcohol.
- Stress, letdown after stress, or emotional strain.
- Bright light, screen exposure, glare, or flicker.
- Strong smells.
- Loud sound.
- Weather or barometric pressure changes.
- Hormonal or menstrual-cycle timing.
- Travel, time-zone change, or disrupted routine.
- Heat, exertion, or environmental changes.
- Neck tension or posture strain.

The pack should give blank lines for individual notes because not every person has the same pattern.

## 5. Common Medication and Treatment Tracking Categories

The product should collect history without suggesting changes. Useful categories:

- Current medications related to migraine.
- Acute/rescue medication names, dose notes if the buyer wants to record them, timing, relief, side effects, and concerns to ask about.
- Preventive medication or treatment history: name, start/stop dates, reason stopped, benefit, side effects.
- Over-the-counter medication use, with a cautious note to discuss frequency with a qualified healthcare professional.
- Non-medication supports: rest, dark room, hydration, caffeine, ice/heat, sleep, anti-nausea support, physical therapy, devices, relaxation practices.
- Supplements or vitamins, if applicable, with provider discussion encouraged.
- Barriers: insurance coverage, cost, refill issues, side effects, trouble taking during aura/nausea, access to appointments.

Do not tell users to start, stop, combine, or adjust medication.

## 6. Doctor-Visit Communication Needs

The appointment pack should help the buyer quickly communicate:

- What changed since the last visit.
- Main reason for the appointment.
- Top three concerns.
- Frequency and duration summary.
- Most disruptive symptoms.
- Current and past treatments tried.
- What has helped, partly helped, not helped, or caused problems.
- How migraine affects work, school, caregiving, driving, social plans, sleep, screen use, and daily tasks.
- Questions they want answered before leaving.
- Decisions or next steps they need clarified.
- Follow-up plan, referrals, prescriptions, tests, instructions, and when/how to contact the office.

AMF guidance emphasizes preparing goals, bringing documentation, documenting symptoms, triggers, medications, and relevant medical history. Mayo Clinic materials also highlight that headache diaries can help healthcare professionals with diagnosis and progress tracking between visits. These support the product's communication-tool framing.

## 7. Buyer Language and Phrases

Useful phrases to include:

- "The main thing I need help with is..."
- "Since my last visit, the biggest change has been..."
- "A typical attack looks like..."
- "The symptoms that worry me most are..."
- "I am missing or reducing..."
- "I have tried..."
- "This helped / partly helped / did not help / caused..."
- "I want to ask about..."
- "Before I leave, I need to understand..."
- "Follow-up notes from the appointment..."

Avoid clinical overreach. Use plain language first, with migraine-specific terms where helpful.

## 8. Existing Product Gaps to Avoid

Avoid these weaknesses:

- Generic appointment planner pages with only "questions" and "notes."
- Too much tiny text or cramped medication tables.
- Prompts that imply the user can identify a single cause of migraine.
- Unsafe treatment language or medication-change suggestions.
- A design that looks like a medical form from a clinic or implies professional endorsement.
- A pack so long that it becomes another task the buyer cannot finish.
- No room for handwriting.
- Missing follow-up notes page.
- Listing copy that promises diagnosis, treatment improvement, or fewer attacks.

## 9. Medical Safety Boundaries

Required disclaimer:

"This printable is a personal tracking and organization tool. It is not medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Always talk with a qualified healthcare professional about symptoms, medications, or care decisions."

Additional safe urgent-symptom language:

"If symptoms feel new, sudden, severe, or concerning, contact a qualified healthcare professional or emergency services."

Medical safety rules:

- Do not claim the pack diagnoses migraine or helps determine whether symptoms are migraine.
- Do not claim it prevents, reduces, treats, or controls attacks.
- Do not tell users when or how to take medication.
- Do not suggest medication changes.
- Do not imply clinician approval.
- Do not provide red-flag triage beyond conservative advice to seek professional or emergency help for urgent concerns.
- Do not frame "triggers" as certain causes.

## 10. Recommended Page List

Recommended 8-page pack:

1. Appointment Snapshot: date, provider, reason for visit, top concerns, quick safety disclaimer.
2. Frequency and Pattern Summary: month/week estimates, duration, phases, timing patterns, recent changes.
3. Symptom Pattern Summary: pain, aura, associated symptoms, most disruptive symptoms, new/different symptoms to discuss.
4. Possible Context and Trigger Notes: cautious categories plus blank notes and "not self-blame" framing.
5. Medication and Treatment History: current, acute, preventive, non-medication supports, side effects, barriers.
6. Impact and Support Needs: work, school, caregiving, daily tasks, screen/light/sound, transportation, help needed.
7. Questions and What I Need Help With: top questions, concerns, decisions, referrals, accommodations, next steps.
8. Follow-Up Notes: provider answers, plan, prescriptions/referrals/tests, next appointment, what to track next.
